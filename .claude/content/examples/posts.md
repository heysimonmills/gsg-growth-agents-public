I made a mistake that almost cost my company over $200,000

A few years ago, I was running a series of new channel experiments, trying to find additional channels to scale beyond Facebook. One of those channels was local TV.

The trend around fractional roles is real and here to stay

Although the idea of a part time or fractional role is not new, in the last couple years its really taken off

Up until recently, a lot companies didn't realize that this type of role even existed!

A lot of the conversations I have generally start with a "How do you typically work with companies?" When I mention the fractional head of growth offering, they want to learn more. Turns out, in many cases, thats exactly the role they are looking for!

What I think is going on here is a case of TAM expansion.

What do I mean by that?

When Uber first launched, investors looked at that and said "ok the TAM for taxis / private car hire is x. Uber needs to capture y% of the market to be successful"

What many didn't realize at the time was that Uber grew the pie for taxis and then took a slice of this larger pie. They got people who drove everywhere to start taking Ubers and increased the market for taxis as a result.

Similarly (and I know this is a stretch), if you were to look at the % of companies open to hiring a part time marketing leader, the % would probably be very low.

But, now with a lot of great talent striking out on their own as fractional leaders and as companies learn more about the offering, the TAM for "part time marketing leader" has grown.

tldr, the fractional role is here to stay!

In the end, what matters is results.

How to turn Out of Home into a performance channel

In my previous post, I talked about how and why you should consider testing OOH as a new performance channel. The final (and arguably most important) piece is measurement.

Measurement can be done in two ways: lift pre/post and direct attribution.

Lift pre/post: as the name suggests, you can look at a few KPIs pre campaign, during and post the campaign to understand what happened.

Those KPIs are 1) avg # of weekly conversions in the given FSA or zip 2) top of funnel metrics like direct/organic website visits, branded search console queries and SEM branded search volume. You could even look at things time on site and change in the % of new vs returning visitors to understand if you're reaching new customers.

Looking at both TOFU metrics as well as comparing conversion numbers will give you a sense of how the campaign performed. For example, even if it didn't drive as many direct conversions as you would've hoped, its still important to understand if you're getting traffic lift.

Direct attribution: depending on the creative and the approach, this can look like promo code redemptions, custom URL visits and conversions (ex. short URL on the board that expands out to a URL with a UTM or simply a custom landing page) as well as a checkout survey. Of the three, the checkout survey is the absolute must have.

By piecing together both pre/post lift analysis as well as direct attribution over time, you can get a really good sense of how your campaign performed.

From there, simply layer on your spend week by week and you'll have a CPA similar to how you would for any other performance channel!

In the past 5 years, I've spent over $1m+ on Out of Home campaigns across North America. A few things I've picked up along the way…

If done correctly, Out of Home can be used as a performance channel with a CAC similar or even lower than "traditional" digital channels like Search and Facebook. It also has the added benefit of increasing brand awareness and brand trust.

There are three factors that make for a successful, performance based OOH campaign:

1️⃣ Budget: you need to spend enough to actually create a lasting impression and give people the time to convert. But if you want to keep your CAC down, you can't spend too much, otherwise you'll oversaturate the market and needlessly drive up your CAC. Budget varies depending on season, geo and placement.

💡 Tip: Running a campaign in Q1 will get you much better bang for your buck vs other times of year. You can stretch your money further with better placements and longer leave up time. Regardless of when you buy, negotiation is critical to getting the best prices and ultimately, the best CAC.

2️⃣ Media Planning: this includes everything from what placements you're going to buy, the actual creative, and where you're going to buy.

Placements: if you're looking to drive conversions, you need high dwell time media with high frequency. Think subway placements, transit shelters, restobar etc combined with high frequency placements like select billboards or taxi tops.

Creative: the simpler the better. For performance based campaigns, something simple and eye catching with a strong CTA, large logo and visible URL, promo code or other prompt for action.

Where to buy: where you buy is critically important. Look at where your best customers live, and target those areas (as narrow as zip code, FSA etc). Alternatively, or additionally, if you have a very specific target demo, you want to blanket the area where they live, work and/or play. You want your target customer to feel like your brand is everywhere and for folks outside your target, that you don't exist.

3️⃣ Measurement: any performance marketer will know that measurement is critical in determining success of a campaign and OOH is no different. Measurement can be done in two ways: lift pre/post and direct attribution. Combined, you can get a pretty good sense for what happened.

Measurement is a big topic so I'll tackle that in a separate post later this week

As you think about your channel mix and how you can deliver incremental growth, consider giving Out of Home a try - you may be surprised by the results 🙂

One of the questions I get most frequently from people looking to try the Digital Nomad life is "where should I stay?"

Over the past year and a half, I've tried all kinds of different arrangements, from Coliving via Outsite, to the hostel + cowork combo of Selina to the classic Airbnb and managed apartment. This is what I've learned.

In a nutshell, places like Selina and Outsite are great for meeting people, but you pay a hefty premium for it. In my opinion, these are great options in small beach or surf towns where they are the centre of social life and a gathering spot for everyone (ex. Sayulita, Playa Venao etc).

In the big cities, particularly nomad hotspots like Buenos Aires, Medellin, Mexico City etc, you're better off in an Airbnb or managed apartment, especially if you're staying 1 month or longer.

Why? Per night, its cheaper and you get more! A full kitchen, bedroom, dining / living space etc. And many places, particularly the managed apartments you can find on sites like Booking.com or directly through Facebook / WhatsApp groups will also offer daily or weekly cleaning service.

But how do you meet people? In these cities, there is already a huge nomad community! You can go to meetups organized via Nomadlist, check Meetup.com, search Facebook for digital nomad in x city, ask around about WhatsApp groups (there are always a ton - foodies, parties, meetups etc) and in very short order you'll have a thriving social life!

This is the approach that I've used and its worked out fairly well! It definitely requires you to be a bit more proactive, but I've found that it strikes the right balance for me!

A few things I've learned:

✅ Building the business is itself a ton of learning and growth, even if it doesn't feel like it at the time
✅ Taking on projects that are a bit outside your comfort zone will push you to level up
✅ Joining a community or mastermind with other likeminded folks (formally or informally) will help you level up on the business side

I'll tell you a story I haven't shared before…how I made $25,000 on the internet as a high school student.

Growing up, I always had an interest in computers and the internet, and I always had a knack for business.

When I was 13, I discovered Wordpress.

I had been going to computer camp and learned the basics of HTML and CSS.

I was also a huge fan of the show House with Hugh Laurie.

I decided to create a fan site with a .wordpress.com domain where I would link to the latest trailers, episode previews and created an episode guide.

On my 14th birthday, my parents got me 1 year of shared hosting with Bluehost so I could move my site from .wordpress.com to its own domain.

I would spend hours linking back to my new website on all the fan forums and tv show forums and websites trying to get people to come visit my site.

I didn't know it at the time, but I was building backlinks along with some basic keyword optimization on the site.

I was ranking #1 on Google for a bunch of different search terms.

I added banner ads, pop ups and signed up for an affiliate program with Commission Junction to sell House t-shirts and merch to monetize the site.

I'll never forget that first cheque for $1.03 that was mailed to me from Google AdSense.

From that moment forward, I was hooked.

My parents thought it was a cute hobby until the cheques started changing from $10 to $1,000.

I would stay up until midnight or 1am the night the latest episode aired so I could get the trailer for the next episode up on the site before anyone else.

I tried the same playbook with two other shows, neither of which took off.

Around the same time, I also dabbled in reselling on eBay, but thats a story for another day.

By the end of first year university as the show was winding down, I had made $25,000.

That money is what paid for some of my living expenses through school as well as my exchange in the Netherlands

That was my first taste of entrepreneurship and making money on the internet and I've been hooked ever since!

What exactly is a Fractional Head of Growth?

I get this question a lot so wanted to break down what this looks like for me as someone who focuses primarily on Seed to Series A B2C

The joy of a role like this is that it can look like whatever the client needs! This is generally a mix of:

1️⃣ Team or contractor management,
2️⃣ Hands on keyboard operational (media buying, email building etc)
3️⃣ Strategy (ex. building projections, setting targets, channel mix, funnel optimization etc).

These three things can flex in and out as needed. Its a super flexible role with the single goal of helping companies grow.

In nutshell, you get a Head of Growth part time for a fraction of the cost of a full time hire!
