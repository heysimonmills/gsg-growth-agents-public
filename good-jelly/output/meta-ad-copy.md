---

**Headline (≤40 chars):**
> It's not the collagen. It's the format.

*(39 chars)*

**CTA button:** Learn More

**Testing hypothesis:** Reframing the #1 objection ("collagen doesn't work") as a format failure neutralises cold-traffic skepticism and positions Good Jelly as the mechanism — not just another supplement. Tests whether dismantling the objection before it's raised drives higher CTR than leading with the pain or the dream.

**Best for:** Cold traffic. Skeptic-adjacent audiences who've dismissed collagen or tried and stopped. Evidence-minded wellness women. Facebook Feed.

Good Jelly Co is different. Not a gummy vitamin. A precision-dosed collagen jelly engineered for women who've done the research, tried the powder, and refused to settle for a product that doesn't keep up with their standards.

---

**Headline (≤40 chars):**
> Not a gummy. A real collagen dose.

*(34 chars)*

**CTA button:** Shop Now

**Testing hypothesis:** Calling out the gummy category's known weakness (underdosing) pre-empts the "it's just candy" objection and creates differentiation for a supplement-literate audience. Tests whether informed skeptics convert at higher rates than general wellness audiences.

**Best for:** Mid-funnel and retargeting. Supplement-aware women who've considered or dismissed collagen gummies. LP visitors who didn't convert.

- [x] Emojis: 0 (appropriate for premium positioning)

## Compliance Note

> ⚠️ This copy has been written to minimise policy risk, but ad platforms update policies
> frequently. Recommend client legal review before launch, especially for health claims.
> Final compliance responsibility rests with the advertiser.

**Flagged for review:**
- **Health category:** Good Jelly is a collagen supplement. All copy falls under Meta's Health & Wellness restricted category.
- **"Supports healthy skin from within"** (Concept 2) — uses permitted "supports" language. Compliant as written, but confirm with legal before launch.
- **Dosing comparison in Concept 4** ("studies supporting skin and joint health typically reference 10–20g daily") — references studies rather than making a direct claim. Confirm brand has substantiation for the dosing figures cited before running.
- **"Guaranteed dose"** — refers to dosing precision (no guessing, no measuring), not a health outcome guarantee. Should be fine, but review in context of full ad set.
- No before/after framing used. No weight or body-measurement language used. No direct "your [condition]" statements used.
